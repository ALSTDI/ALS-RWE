    The STARDUSTT Framework Addresses the Curse of Knowledge in Clinical Informatics

    Authors: Danielle Boyce, Pavel Goriacko, William M Garneau, Pamela Dasher, William Roddy, Wesley Anderson, Smith F. Heavner
    Corresponding Author: Danielle Boyce
    Affiliations:
    - ALS Therapy Development Institute
    - Johns Hopkins University School of Medicine
    - Tufts University School of Medicine
    - Montefiore Einstein, Center for Health Data Innovations
    - Critical Path Institute
    - Clemson University, Department of Public Health Sciences

    Abstract

    With the increasing adoption of standardized data frameworks, many healthcare centers are transforming electronic health records into common data models (CDMs) such as the Observational Medical Outcomes Partnership (OMOP). The OMOP extract–transform–load (ETL) process is complex and necessitates collaboration among data engineers, clinical informaticists, clinicians, and health system leaders—groups with widely varying technical backgrounds and obligations. A recurring challenge is translating technical knowledge across these diverse groups, often hindered by the curse of knowledge.

    We describe best practices for coordinating a large, multi-institution OMOP project and propose the STARDUSTT framework—Secure Data, Technology Awareness, Active Clinical Representation, Relational Databases, Data Quality, Utilization of GitHub, Standardized Vocabularies, Training & Documentation, Translation & Communication—as a structured approach to ensure implementation readiness across sites.

    Keywords: OMOP, OHDSI, observational research, ETL, standardized vocabularies

    Background

    Large-scale OMOP CDM initiatives such as the National COVID Cohort Collaborative (N3C), the All of Us Research Program, and the European Health Data & Evidence Network (EHDEN) have raised the visibility of observational research and data harmonization. In the CURE ID project, which supported sites across the United States, Asia, and South America, we observed consistent foundational knowledge gaps between stakeholder groups.

    Participating effectively in OMOP projects requires more than knowledge of the CDM schema. Teams must also understand: - Principles of observational research
    - Standardized terminologies and concept hierarchies
    - Programming languages (e.g., SQL, R, Python)
    - Tooling (e.g., GitHub, Docker, OHDSI software stack)
    - Relational database concepts and DDL
    - Basics of privacy-preserving data sharing

    Many clinical and informatics leaders trained before the advent of modern real-world data workflows, open-source analytics, and DevOps-style collaboration tools—creating real barriers to onboarding without structured support.

    Methods

    More than 30 hospital sites were recruited to participate in an OMOP implementation initiative. After initial buy-in, each site underwent a structured evaluation to confirm readiness. Qualified sites met regularly with the technical team to work through: - Concept mapping and vocabulary alignment
    - ETL script design and development
    - OHDSI tooling setup and use
    - De-identification protocols and export procedures

    Data quality was assessed using automated checks (e.g., conformance, completeness, plausibility) to provide oversight of the transferred data. We used the EPIS framework (Exploration, Preparation, Implementation, Sustainment) to guide ongoing process evaluation and to adapt our approach based on stakeholder feedback. Interim findings were presented at the 16th Annual Conference on Dissemination and Implementation Science.

    Challenges included time and resource constraints, regulatory considerations, and cross-disciplinary communication barriers. We conducted key informant interviews at institutions that declined to participate and at institutions that adopted OMOP processes. We compared emergent conceptual models to CFIR and RE-AIM and, through member checking and iterative synthesis of project notes, developed the STARDUSTT framework to guide future implementations.

    The STARDUSTT Framework


    Figure 1. Overview of the STARDUSTT framework.

    The framework addresses nine capabilities necessary for OMOP implementation readiness:

    1. Secure Data — Practical training in de-identification and PHI protection; robust security practices for secondary use of EHR data.
    2. Technology Awareness — Familiarity with Docker, command line, SQL, and OHDSI tooling; understanding of local SQL dialects and infrastructure.
    3. Active Clinical Representation — Interdisciplinary participation from physicians, nurses, and other clinicians to ensure clinical context, provenance, and priorities are reflected in technical decisions.
    4. Relational Databases — Foundations in schema design, indexing, joins, and SQL querying; understanding OMOP table relationships.
    5. Data Quality — Routine checks for conformance, completeness, and plausibility; processes to triage and remediate issues before data sharing.
    6. Utilization of GitHub — Version control, issue tracking, and collaborative workflows to manage ETL code, documentation, and change control.
    7. Standardized Vocabularies — SNOMED CT, LOINC, RxNorm, and use of Athena; local-to-standard mapping practices aligned with OHDSI conventions.
    8. Training & Documentation — Protocols, SOPs, MOPs/MOOs, step-by-step guides, and curated learning paths; emphasis on reproducibility and standardization.
    9. Translation & Communication — Clear, audience-specific messaging that turns technical jargon into plain language; bidirectional feedback; consistent terminology; shared artifacts (infographics, glossaries, executive summaries) to maintain alignment across leadership, clinical, and engineering teams.

    The Curse of Knowledge and Its Impact on OMOP Projects

    A root cause of many misalignments is the curse of knowledge—the tendency of experts to overestimate what others know, which hinders instruction and communication (Camerer, Loewenstein, & Weber, 1989). In OMOP implementations, leaders and developers who are fluent in open-source tooling (Docker, GitHub, OHDSI stack) may not appreciate prerequisite gaps in database fundamentals, command line usage, or data governance. Mitigating this bias requires: - Simplifying explanations and actively checking for understanding
    - Using consistent language and shared glossaries
    - Tailoring content to personas (executives, clinical informaticists, engineers)
    - Providing incremental learning with real-world, hands-on exercises

    The STARDUSTT framework operationalizes these practices so that sites develop the skills—and shared language—required to implement and sustain OMOP successfully.

    Problem Impact Strategy
    Team members have varying levels of expertise Leaders have difficulty making decisions Level the playing field with foundational training

    Jumping into complex topics too quickly Frustration and disengagement Build knowledge incrementally with scaffolding
    Passive, asynchronous learning only Lower retention and skill transfer Incorporate active learning and hands-on exercises
    Infrequent feedback loops Persistent misunderstandings; slow progress Schedule regular check-ins and feedback cycles
    One-size-fits-all instruction Inconsistent outcomes across roles Diversify teaching methods to match personas
    Incomplete or unclear documentation Poor reproducibility; limited autonomy Provide clear, accessible, versioned documentation
    Isolated learning Slower problem-solving and innovation Encourage mentorship and peer-to-peer learning
    Emphasis on theory without practice Difficulty applying knowledge Use real-world case studies and ETL labs
    Static training programs Outdated practices and tools Continuously refine and update materials

    Understanding Personas

    C-Suite Executives. Focus on strategic overviews, risk management, compliance, ROI, and resourcing. Provide non-technical summaries and clear decision points.

    Physician/Nurse Clinical Informaticists. Often serve as PIs or project leaders; require a working knowledge of EHR schema, standardized terminologies, IRB/privacy, and OHDSI workflows. Training should be time-efficient and jargon-light, with practical examples.

    Data Engineers. Need detailed instruction in relational databases, SQL dialects, OMOP versions, GitHub workflows, and containerized deployments. Provide project context along with technical depth to avoid “building in the dark.”

    By tailoring the STARDUSTT framework to these personas, teams align strategy, clinical reality, and technical execution.

    Conclusion

    “If you’ve seen one hospital, you’ve seen one hospital.” OMOP implementations succeed when training and communication are tailored to diverse roles. By recognizing and countering the curse of knowledge, teams can provide clear, incremental, and practical training that builds shared understanding and capability. The STARDUSTT framework offers a structured, adaptable approach to develop implementation readiness and sustain high-quality participation in OHDSI/OMOP projects.
